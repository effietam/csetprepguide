CSET Spanish Subtest II 30-Day Study Plan

📅 Day-by-day schedule 📖 All 3 domains covered ✍️ Essay practice built in 🆓 100% free 📅 Updated 2026

This day-by-day 30-day study plan covers all three Subtest II content domains — Spanish literature, Latin American literature, and culture and civilization — with constructed-response writing practice and diagnostic checkpoints built in at the right moments. Follow it sequentially, or use the weekly goals to adapt it to your own timeline.

📅 Last updated: June 2026 ⏱️ 1–2 hrs/day 🎯 Test Code 146

📌 How to Use This Plan

  • Each day assumes 1–2 hours of focused, active study — not passive re-reading.
  • Take the Subtest II Practice Test diagnostically on Day 7 and as a full benchmark on Day 28.
  • Constructed-response writing practice is scheduled on Days 13, 20, and 27 — do not skip these.
  • Every Sunday is a review day — use it to consolidate the week's content, not to introduce new material.
  • If a week's content feels familiar, compress it to 3–4 days and redistribute time to weaker areas.
  • Day 30 is light — review only, no new content, early sleep.

Week 1 — Spanish Literary History (Days 1–7)

📖 Week 1: Spanish Literature from Medieval to 19th Century

Days 1–7
Weekly goal: Build a solid timeline of Spanish literary history from the Cantar de Mio Cid through Realism and Naturalism. Know the defining features of each period and its key authors before moving on. End the week with a diagnostic practice test of Domain A questions.
Day1
Medieval and Pre-Renaissance Spain
Cantar de Mio Cid (c. 1200) — structure, hero, themes of honor and exile · Libro de buen amor (Juan Ruiz) · Alfonso X "El Sabio" and vernacular prose · mester de juglaría vs. mester de clerecía traditions
Lit
Day2
Renaissance: Garcilaso & La Celestina
Garcilaso de la Vega — Italian sonnet, pastoral eclogue, Églogas · Fernando de Rojas: La Celestina (1499) — tragicomedy in dialogue, social realism, moral ambiguity · picaresque genre origins: Lazarillo de Tormes (1554, anonymous)
Lit
Day3
Golden Age: Cervantes
Don Quixote Parts I (1605) & II (1615) — metafiction, unreliable narrator, parody of chivalric romance, Sancho Panza as foil · significance as first modern novel · Cervantes's other works: Novelas ejemplares
Lit
Day4
Golden Age: Theater & Baroque Poetry
Lope de Vega: comedia nueva, Arte nuevo, Fuenteovejuna · Calderón: La vida es sueño, El alcalde de Zalamea, honor plays · Góngora: culteranismo, Soledades · Quevedo: conceptismo, satirical sonnets — know the culteranismo vs. conceptismo contrast cold
Lit
Day5
18th & 19th Century: Enlightenment, Romanticism, Realism
Jovellanos and the Spanish Enlightenment (light coverage) · Bécquer: Rimas and Leyendas — Romanticism · Espronceda: El estudiante de Salamanca · Pérez Galdós: Fortunata y Jacinta, Episodios nacionales — Realism · Emilia Pardo Bazán and Spanish Naturalism
Lit
Day6
Naturalism & Clarín
Leopoldo Alas "Clarín": La Regenta (1884–85) — Spanish Naturalism, provincial society, comparison to Madame Bovary · Juan Valera: Pepita Jiménez — Realism · Review Days 1–5: write a one-sentence description of each period and its defining characteristics
Lit
Day7
Diagnostic: Domain A Practice Test
Take the Spanish Literature section of the practice test — note every wrong answer · Review all explanations including correct ones · Flag any literary period or author where you missed 2+ questions — that becomes extra study time next week · Do not advance to new content today
Test

Week 2 — 20th-Century Spain + Colonial Latin America (Days 8–14)

🌎 Week 2: Generation of '98, Generation of '27 & Colonial Latin America

Days 8–14
Weekly goal: Know both Spanish Generations (and how to tell them apart), understand the Civil War's devastating impact on the '27 Generation, and establish the colonial Latin American foundation including Sor Juana and early independence writing. Write your first timed constructed response on Day 13.
Day8
Generation of '98: Unamuno & Azorín
Historical context: Spain's 1898 defeat — Cuba, Puerto Rico, Philippines · Unamuno: intrahistoria, Del sentimiento trágico de la vida, Niebla — existential philosophy · Azorín: impressionist Castilian prose, Castilla — prose style vs. content · intrahistoria concept in depth
Lit
Day9
Generation of '98: Machado, Baroja & Novecentismo
Antonio Machado: Campos de Castilla, Soledades — Castilian landscape, contemplative poetry · Pío Baroja: pessimistic realist novels · Novecentismo contrast: Ortega y Gasset, Juan Ramón Jiménez — rationalist, not nationalist · How to distinguish Gen. '98 from Novecentismo on the exam
Lit
Day10
Generation of '27: Lorca & the Civil War
García Lorca in depth: rural tragedy trilogy (Bodas de sangre, Yerma, La casa de Bernarda Alba); Romancero gitano; duende theory; the romance ballad form (octosyllabic, assonant rhyme) · Lorca's 1936 execution · Generation of '27 founding: 300th anniversary of Góngora's death
Lit
Day11
Generation of '27: The Exile Poets & Postwar Spain
Alberti, Guillén, Salinas, Cernuda — all exiled after Civil War · Vicente Aleixandre (Nobel 1977) stayed in Spain · Postwar fiction: Camilo José Cela — tremendismo, La familia de Pascual Duarte (1942) · Carmen Laforet: Nada (1945) · Social realism of 1950s–60s
Lit
Day12
Colonial Latin America: Sor Juana & Las Casas
Sor Juana Inés de la Cruz (1648–1695): first great poet of the Americas, "Tenth Muse," Respuesta a Sor Filotea (early feminist defense), Baroque conceptismo, sonnets, Primero sueño · Bartolomé de las Casas: Brevísima relación de la destrucción de las Indias — indigenous rights · Colonial literary context
LatAm
Day13
✍️ Constructed Response #1 + Modernismo
Morning (45 min): Timed CR essay — topic: "Discuss how the Spanish Civil War shaped the work of one member of the Generation of '27." Write for 25 min, review and score for 10 min · Afternoon: Rubén Darío: Modernismo, Azul... (1888), Prosas profanas (1896), French Symbolism influence, renovation of the Spanish language
CR
Day14
Week 2 Review
Review all Week 2 content · Create a comparison chart: Gen. '98 vs. Gen. '27 (dates, key authors, defining context, major works) · Review your Day 13 CR essay — did you use specific titles, dates, and authors? Rewrite any section that was vague · Check Day 7 flagged authors — are those gaps now closed?
Review

Week 3 — Latin American 20th Century (Days 15–21)

🌎 Week 3: Boom, Magical Realism & the Full Latin American Canon

Days 15–21
Weekly goal: Master magical realism's precise definition and its precursors, know all four core Boom authors in depth, and cover Borges, Neruda, Paz, and Mistral. Write your second timed constructed response on Day 20. End the week with a full Domain B diagnostic.
Day15
Criollismo, Negrismo & Pre-Boom Movements
Criollismo: Gallegos (Doña Bárbara), Rivera (La vorágine), Güiraldes (Don Segundo Sombra) — know the defining features · Negrismo: Nicolás Guillén — Afro-Cuban rhythms, Sóngoro cosongo (1931), Harlem Renaissance parallel · Gabriela Mistral: first Latin American Nobel (1945), maternal themes, Desolación
LatAm
Day16
Borges
Jorge Luis Borges: Ficciones (1944), El Aleph (1949) — labyrinths, mirrors, infinite time, metafiction · "El jardín de senderos que se bifurcan" — forking time · "La biblioteca de Babel" — infinite knowledge · Borges and Ultraísmo (brought from Spain to Argentina 1921) · His relationship to and influence on the Boom
LatAm
Day17
Neruda & Octavio Paz
Neruda's three phases: (1) love poetry — Veinte poemas (1924); (2) Surrealism — Residencia en la tierra; (3) political epic — Canto general; Odas elementales — democratic materialist poetics · Nobel 1971 · Paz: El laberinto de la soledad (1950) — Mexican identity, la Chingada, masks, fiesta · Nobel 1990
LatAm
Day18
Magical Realism: Precursors & Definition
Alejo Carpentier: lo real maravilloso americano — El reino de este mundo (1949) prologue · Juan Rulfo: Pedro Páramo (1955) — dead narrator, Comala village, García Márquez's acknowledgment · Magical realism definition: magical events in realistic setting, narrated without wonder, culturally specific · Magical realism ≠ fantasy — practice the distinction with three examples
LatAm
Day19
The Latin American Boom
García Márquez: Cien años de soledad (1967) — Macondo, Buendía family, circular time, temporal folding in opening sentence; Nobel 1982 · Vargas Llosa: La ciudad y los perros (1963) — Lima military academy; Nobel 2010 · Cortázar: Rayuela (1963) — reader-chosen chapter order, anti-novel structure · Fuentes: La muerte de Artemio Cruz — Mexican Revolution, fragmented narrative
LatAm
Day20
✍️ Constructed Response #2 + Post-Boom
Morning (45 min): Timed CR essay — topic: "Define magical realism and explain how García Márquez's Cien años de soledad exemplifies it." Write 25 min, self-score 10 min · Afternoon: Post-Boom: Allende (La casa de los espíritus), Bolaño (2666), McOndo movement · Testimonio genre: Rigoberta Menchú
CR
Day21
Diagnostic: Domain B Practice Test
Take the Latin American Literature section of the practice test · Review every explanation · Flag any author, movement, or distinction you missed · Compare your Domain B score to Domain A score — your weaker domain gets priority in Week 4 early days · Note any cultural questions that appeared in the literary sections
Test

Week 4 — Culture, Civilization & Final Review (Days 22–30)

🏛️ Week 4: Culture, Civilization, Art, Music & Full Benchmark

Days 22–30
Weekly goal: Cover the entire Culture & Civilization domain — the most commonly underestimated section. Take the full 100-question benchmark test on Day 28, use Day 29 for targeted remediation, and Day 30 for light review only. Your exam should be scheduled the day after Day 30.
Day22
History of Spain
Moorish period and convivencia · Reconquista (718–1492) — fall of Granada, Columbus, expulsion of Jews — all 1492 · Spanish Inquisition (1478) — primary targets: conversos and moriscos · Colonial empire · Spanish-American War 1898 · Civil War 1936–39 · Franco 1939–75 · 1978 Constitution — autonomous communities (Catalan, Basque, Galician)
Civ
Day23
Pre-Columbian Civilizations
Aztec: Tenochtitlán (1325 CE), Nahuatl, the calendar, encomienda system · Maya: Popol Vuh — K'iche' creation myth, three failed attempts (mud → wood → maize), Hero Twins, maize as human material; Dresden Codex; advanced astronomy · Inca: Machu Picchu, quipu (knotted cord records), mita labor system, ayllu kinship unit, Quechua
Civ
Day24
Colonial Latin America & Independence
Encomienda — labor extraction, Las Casas's opposition, New Laws of 1542 · Mestizaje — Vasconcelos's raza cósmica · Independence leaders: Bolívar (Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, Bolivia), San Martín (Argentina, Chile), Hidalgo (Mexico — Grito de Dolores 1810) · Mexican Revolution 1910–20 · José Martí: Nuestra América (1891)
Civ
Day25
Art of Spain & Latin America
Velázquez: Las Meninas (1656) — Baroque court painter, perspective · Goya: court portraits → war paintings (El 3 de mayo) → Black Paintings (Saturno devorando a su hijo) → proto-Expressionism · Picasso: Cubism, Guernica (1937) — Nazi-Fascist bombing of Basque town · Gaudí: Catalan Modernisme, Sagrada Família · Rivera + Orozco + Siqueiros: Mexican muralism · Frida Kahlo: self-portraits, retablo style
Civ
Day26
Music, Traditions & Cultural Concepts
Flamenco: cante, baile, toque; Roma, Moorish, Jewish, Spanish folk roots; UNESCO 2010 · Tango: Buenos Aires/Montevideo working-class immigrant neighborhoods, 1880s–90s · Cumbia: Colombian Caribbean coast, African + indigenous + Spanish · Mariachi: Mexican folk/ceremonial · Día de los Muertos: Aztec + Catholic synthesis · Machismo/marianismo gender ideologies
Civ
Day27
✍️ Constructed Response #3 + Geography Review
Morning (45 min): Timed CR essay — topic: "Discuss how Mexican muralism functioned as public education after the Revolution." Write 25 min, self-score 10 min · Afternoon: Geography — Spain's autonomous communities and capitals; Latin American countries, capitals, and key cultural associations; geography questions in practice test
CR
Day28
🎯 Full 100-Question Benchmark Test
Take the complete Subtest II Practice Test — all 100 questions, timed, no interruptions, no looking things up · Score yourself by domain (Domains A, B, C separately) · Target: 78%+ in each domain · Record every missed question with its domain and topic — this list drives Day 29
Test
Day29
Targeted Remediation
Work through every missed question from Day 28 · Re-study the content behind each wrong answer — not just the answer, but the full concept · Any domain below 70% gets 60 min of focused re-study using the study guide · Any domain 70–78% gets 30 min · Domains above 78% — read explanations only, no re-study needed
Review
Day30
Light Review Only — Rest Day
Review your comparison charts and flashcards from earlier weeks — 30–45 min maximum · Read through your three constructed-response drafts one final time · Do not study new content · No practice test · Eat well, sleep early — your preparation is complete
Review

Adjusting the Plan to Your Background

This plan is designed for candidates with a moderate Spanish background needing full domain coverage. If your situation differs, here is how to adapt it.

📗 Literature / Humanities Degree

Take the Domain A diagnostic on Day 1. If you score 80%+, compress Week 1 to 3 days and use the saved time for extra Domain C study in Week 4. Most of your effort should go to art history and pre-Columbian civilizations.

🗣️ Language / Linguistics Degree

Follow the plan as written. Give extra attention to Days 3–4 (Golden Age) and Days 18–19 (magical realism + Boom) — these are typically the biggest gaps for language-background candidates.

⏱️ Less Than 30 Days

Prioritize in this order: Golden Age (Day 3–4), Boom + magical realism (Day 18–19), Sor Juana (Day 12), Culture domain (Days 22–26). Cut rest days and compress Week 1 reviews if needed. Always keep the two CR practice sessions.

📅 More Than 30 Days

Add a fifth week before Week 1 for pre-reading primary texts: excerpts from Don Quixote, Lorca's poetry, Cien años de soledad, and Borges. Reading actual texts (not summaries) improves both literary analysis and constructed-response quality significantly.

Don't skip the constructed-response practice days. Days 13, 20, and 27 are easy to skip when you feel behind on content — but the essays are weighted heavily in the final scaled score. Three timed essays before exam day is the minimum. Candidates who practice writing consistently outperform those who rely on content knowledge alone.
