What Is CSET Spanish Subtest II?

CSET Spanish Subtest II (Test Code 146) is the second of three subtests required for the California Single Subject Teaching Credential in Spanish. While Subtest I tests your command of the Spanish language itself — grammar, vocabulary, reading, and oral communication — Subtest II tests what you know about Spanish-language literature and culture.

You are expected to demonstrate the knowledge a qualified Spanish teacher needs to contextualize literary works for students, discuss the cultural traditions of Spanish-speaking countries, and analyze texts from multiple literary periods and movements. The exam is administered entirely through selected-response (multiple-choice) questions and two constructed-response prompts answered in English.

Critical distinction: Subtest II is not a language test. You will not be asked to conjugate verbs, correct grammar errors, or write in Spanish. All constructed-response answers are written in English. If you have been studying grammar for Subtest I, that knowledge does not transfer here — you need to shift into literature and cultural history mode.

Domain Breakdown: What the Exam Covers

Subtest II is organized into three broad content domains. Understanding the weight of each domain helps you allocate your study time efficiently.

📖 Domain A ~33%

Spanish Literature

Literary history of Spain from the medieval period through the 20th century, including major authors, works, and movements.

  • Medieval and Renaissance literature
  • Golden Age (Siglo de Oro)
  • Romanticism and Realism
  • Generation of '98 and '27
  • 20th-century Spanish fiction and poetry
  • Literary analysis and devices

🌎 Domain B ~33%

Latin American Literature

Colonial-era writing through the contemporary period, including the Boom, magical realism, and major authors across the region.

  • Colonial literature and Sor Juana
  • Modernismo (Rubén Darío)
  • Criollismo and regionalism
  • The Latin American Boom
  • Magical realism
  • Post-Boom and contemporary voices

🏛️ Domain C ~33%

Culture & Civilization

History, geography, art, music, and cultural traditions of Spain and Latin America, plus connections between language and culture.

  • History of Spain (Reconquista through Franco)
  • Pre-Columbian civilizations
  • Colonial history and independence
  • Art: Velázquez, Goya, Picasso, Rivera, Kahlo
  • Music: flamenco, tango, cumbia, mariachi
  • Traditions, religion, and geography

Study tip: Each domain carries roughly equal weight, which means you cannot afford to ignore any of the three. Many candidates over-invest in literature and underestimate how much culture and civilization knowledge the exam tests — geography, art history, and pre-Columbian civilizations included.

Domain A — Spanish Literature: What You Need to Know

Medieval Period (c. 1100–1400)

Know the Cantar de Mio Cid (c. 1200) as the founding text of Spanish literature — its realistic hero, epic structure, and themes of honor and exile. Understand the mester de clerecía and mester de juglaría traditions. Gonzalo de Berceo's religious poetry and Juan Ruiz's Libro de buen amor are also frequently tested. Know Alfonso X "El Sabio" as the patron of vernacular prose.

Renaissance and the Transition to the Golden Age (c. 1400–1580)

Garcilaso de la Vega introduced the Italian sonnet and pastoral eclogue to Spanish poetry — the most important Renaissance literary figure. Fernando de Rojas's La Celestina (1499) bridges medieval and Renaissance literature as a tragicomedy in dialogue form. Know the picaresque genre: Lazarillo de Tormes (1554, anonymous) is the founding text — low-born narrator, social satire, ironic first-person voice.

The Golden Age — Siglo de Oro (c. 1580–1680)

This is the most heavily tested literary period on Subtest II. You need to know:

  • Miguel de Cervantes: Don Quixote (Parts I and II) — first modern novel, metafiction, parody of chivalric romances, Sancho Panza as foil
  • Lope de Vega: Mass theater (comedia nueva), Arte nuevo de hacer comedias, mixing of tragic and comic, Fuenteovejuna
  • Pedro Calderón de la Barca: La vida es sueño (illusion vs. reality), El alcalde de Zalamea (honor), Baroque philosophical theater
  • Luis de Góngora: Culteranismo — Latinized vocabulary, dense metaphor, Soledades, Fábula de Polifemo y Galatea
  • Francisco de Quevedo: Conceptismo — wit, paradox, wordplay, Sueños, satirical sonnets

Know the difference between culteranismo (Góngora) and conceptismo (Quevedo) — this distinction appears on nearly every administration of the exam. Also know the honor (comedia de honor) and limpieza de sangre themes in Golden Age drama.

18th and 19th Century: Enlightenment, Romanticism, Realism, Naturalism

The Enlightenment is lightly tested — know Jovellanos and the general move toward reason and reform. Romanticism centers on Gustavo Adolfo Bécquer (Rimas — intimate love poetry; Leyendas — Gothic prose) and José de Espronceda (El estudiante de Salamanca). The key Realist novels are Benito Pérez Galdós (Fortunata y Jacinta, Episodios nacionales) and Leopoldo Alas "Clarín" (La Regenta — Naturalism, provincial society). Emilia Pardo Bazán introduced French Naturalism to Spain.

Generation of '98 and Early 20th Century

The Generation of '98 responded to Spain's loss of Cuba, Puerto Rico, and the Philippines in 1898 with philosophical reflection on Spanish identity. Key figures: Miguel de Unamuno (intrahistoria, Del sentimiento trágico de la vida, Niebla), Azorín (impressionist prose portraits of Castile), Pío Baroja (pessimistic realist novels), Antonio Machado (Campos de Castilla — the defining poetry of the generation). Distinguish the Generation of '98 from Novecentismo (Ortega y Gasset, Juan Ramón Jiménez — rationalist, classicist, less nationalist).

Generation of '27 and the Spanish Civil War

Named for the 300th anniversary of Góngora's death, this generation produced the peak of 20th-century Spanish poetry. The Civil War devastated it: Federico García Lorca was executed in 1936; the rest (Rafael Alberti, Jorge Guillén, Pedro Salinas, Luis Cernuda, Vicente Aleixandre) went into exile. Know Lorca's rural tragedy trilogy (Bodas de sangre, Yerma, La casa de Bernarda Alba), his concept of duende, and his Romancero gitano. Know the romance ballad form (octosyllabic lines, assonant rhyme).

Post-Civil War and Late 20th Century

Camilo José Cela (La familia de Pascual Duarte) founded tremendismo. Carmen Laforet's Nada is another postwar landmark. Know social realism and the novela social of the 1950s–60s. For the contemporary period, know that Spain's transition to democracy after Franco's death (1975) enabled a literary and cultural renaissance.

Domain B — Latin American Literature: What You Need to Know

Colonial Literature

Sor Juana Inés de la Cruz (1648–1695) is the most important colonial figure and one of the most frequently tested authors on the entire exam. Know her as the first great poet of the Americas, an early feminist defender of women's right to education (Respuesta a Sor Filotea de la Cruz), and a master of Baroque conceptismo. Know her sonnets and the Primero sueño. Bartolomé de las Casas's Brevísima relación is the key colonial-era prose text about indigenous rights.

19th Century: Independence and Modernismo

José Martí (Cuba) is the key 19th-century figure — poet, independence leader, and essayist (Nuestra América). Rubén Darío (Nicaragua) founded Modernismo — Latin America's first truly original literary movement, drawing on French Symbolism and Parnassianism. Know Azul... (1888) and Prosas profanas (1896) as his defining works. Modernismo renovated the Spanish language itself through musical verse and sensory imagery.

Early 20th Century: Criollismo, Negrismo, and the Vanguard

Criollismo (regionalism) emphasized rural landscapes and local customs — key texts are Rómulo Gallegos's Doña Bárbara, José Eustasio Rivera's La vorágine, and Ricardo Güiraldes's Don Segundo Sombra. Negrismo affirmed Afro-Caribbean culture and heritage — Nicolás Guillén (Cuba) is the central poet, using Afro-Cuban rhythms and vocabulary (Sóngoro cosongo). Gabriela Mistral (Chile) won the Nobel Prize in 1945 — the first Latin American to do so. Know her themes: maternal love, death, and nature.

The Latin American Boom (1960s–1970s)

The Boom is the most heavily tested period in Latin American literature. Know all four core Boom authors in detail:

  • Gabriel García Márquez (Colombia): Cien años de soledad — founding text of magical realism, Macondo, Buendía family, circular time, Nobel Prize 1982
  • Mario Vargas Llosa (Peru): La ciudad y los perros (Lima military academy), complex structure, Nobel Prize 2010
  • Julio Cortázar (Argentina): Rayuela — reader-chosen chapter order, anti-novel, labyrinthine structure
  • Carlos Fuentes (Mexico): La muerte de Artemio Cruz — Mexican Revolution, fragmented time, multiple narrators

Magical Realism — the Essential Definition

Magical realism is not fantasy fiction. The key distinction: magical events are presented as ordinary, matter-of-fact occurrences within an otherwise realistic social and political setting. The magic has cultural meaning specific to the author's community (Caribbean oral tradition, indigenous cosmology). Alejo Carpentier's concept of lo real maravilloso americano (the marvelous reality of the Americas) preceded and influenced García Márquez's magical realism. Juan Rulfo's Pedro Páramo (1955) is the crucial precursor García Márquez credited directly.

Other Essential Authors

Jorge Luis Borges (Argentina): Ficciones, El Aleph — labyrinths, mirrors, infinite time, metafiction. Not a Boom author — predates and influences the Boom. Pablo Neruda (Chile): three phases — early love poetry (Veinte poemas), Surrealist period (Residencia en la tierra), political/epic poetry (Canto general). Nobel Prize 1971. Octavio Paz (Mexico): El laberinto de la soledad (Mexican identity essay), Nobel Prize 1990. Isabel Allende (Chile): La casa de los espíritus — magical realism, feminist focus, Pinochet coup. Rigoberta Menchú (Guatemala): Me llamo Rigoberta Menchú — the testimonio genre, Nobel Peace Prize 1992.

Domain C — Culture & Civilization: What You Need to Know

History of Spain

Know these key historical turning points: the Reconquista (718–1492) — Christian kingdoms reclaiming Iberia from Moorish rule; 1492 — Columbus, fall of Granada, and the expulsion of Jews (all the same year; know that connection); the Spanish Inquisition (1478) — primarily targeted conversos and moriscos; Spain's colonial empire and its decline; the Spanish-American War of 1898 and loss of Cuba, Puerto Rico, and the Philippines (catalyst for the Generation of '98); the Spanish Civil War (1936–1939) and Franco's dictatorship (1939–1975); Spain's transition to democracy and the system of autonomous communities (1978 Constitution). Know the regional languages — Catalan, Basque, Galician — and their cultural significance.

Pre-Columbian Civilizations and Colonial Latin America

Know the three major pre-Columbian civilizations in enough depth to answer questions about their cultural contributions: the Aztecs (Tenochtitlán, 1325 CE; the Templo Mayor; the calendar; Nahuatl language); the Maya (Popol Vuh — K'iche' creation myth with maize as the material of humanity; the Hero Twins; the Dresden Codex; advanced astronomy); the Inca (Machu Picchu; the quipu knotted cord record-keeping; the mita labor system; Quechua). Know the encomienda and mita labor systems and the role of Bartolomé de las Casas in opposing them.

Latin American Independence and Modern History

Know the key independence leaders: Simón Bolívar (Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, Bolivia — "El Libertador"), José de San Martín (Argentina, Chile, Peru), Miguel Hidalgo (Mexico — the Grito de Dolores, 1810). Know the concept of mestizaje (racial and cultural mixing) and its celebration by José Vasconcelos (La raza cósmica). Know the Mexican Revolution (1910–1920) and its cultural legacy — muralism, land reform, nationalism.

Art: Spain and Latin America

These artists appear on the exam regularly:

  • Diego Velázquez — Spanish Baroque court painter, Las Meninas (1656), extraordinary use of perspective and light
  • Francisco Goya — transition from Baroque to Romanticism; court portraits; war paintings (El 3 de mayo de 1808); Black Paintings (Saturno devorando a su hijo)
  • Pablo Picasso — Cubism; Guernica (1937) as protest against the Nazi-Fascist bombing of the Basque town during the Spanish Civil War
  • Antoni Gaudí — Catalan Modernisme (Art Nouveau); Sagrada Família, Casa Batlló, Park Güell
  • Diego Rivera — Mexican Muralism; National Palace murals; Marxist ideology; married to Frida Kahlo
  • Frida Kahlo — self-portraits; physical pain and identity; Mexican folk art (retablo style); feminist icon
  • José Clemente Orozco & David Alfaro Siqueiros — the other two of the "Big Three" Mexican muralists

Music and Performing Arts

Know flamenco (Andalusia: cante, baile, toque; Roma, Moorish, Jewish, and Spanish folk roots; UNESCO heritage). Know the origins and key features of: tango (Buenos Aires and Montevideo working-class immigrant neighborhoods; African candombe + Cuban habanera + European polka); cumbia (Caribbean coast of Colombia; African, indigenous, and Spanish elements); mariachi (Mexican folk/ceremonial music); salsa (Caribbean/New York fusion). Know Spanish classical composers Isaac Albéniz and Manuel de Falla.

Cultural Traditions and Concepts

These cultural concepts frequently appear on the exam: mestizaje (racial and cultural mixing); machismo and marianismo (gender role ideologies); personalismo (value of personal relationships); familismo (centrality of family); Día de los Muertos (Aztec + Catholic synthesis, Nov. 1–2); the Reconquista and its legacy of convivencia (coexistence) and conflict between Christians, Muslims, and Jews; Spain's autonomous communities and regional identities (Catalonia, Basque Country, Galicia).

30-Day Study Plan

This plan assumes roughly 1–2 hours of study per day. Adjust based on your existing background — if you have a humanities or Spanish literature degree, you may need less time on literary periods and can redirect effort to cultural history and art.

Week 1 Spanish Literary History (Days 1–7)

  • Day 1: Medieval Spain — Cantar de Mio Cid, Libro de buen amor, La Celestina
  • Day 2: Renaissance — Garcilaso de la Vega, Italian forms, pastoral poetry
  • Day 3: Golden Age overview — Cervantes and Don Quixote (metafiction, structure, themes)
  • Day 4: Golden Age theater — Lope de Vega, Calderón, honor plays, Arte nuevo
  • Day 5: Baroque poetry — Góngora (culteranismo) vs. Quevedo (conceptismo); picaresque genre
  • Day 6: 19th century — Romanticism (Bécquer), Realism (Galdós), Naturalism (Clarín, Pardo Bazán)
  • Day 7: Review & self-quiz — take the Domain A section of the practice test

Week 2 20th-Century Spain + Colonial Latin America (Days 8–14)

  • Day 8: Generation of '98 — Unamuno (intrahistoria), Machado (Campos de Castilla), Azorín
  • Day 9: Generation of '27 — Lorca (plays, duende, romance form), Alberti, Guillén, Salinas
  • Day 10: Civil War impact on literature; postwar tremendismo (Cela); social realism
  • Day 11: Colonial Latin American literature — Sor Juana (full deep dive), Bartolomé de las Casas
  • Day 12: 19th-century Latin America — Martí, independence literature, the essay tradition
  • Day 13: Modernismo — Rubén Darío, French Symbolism connection, defining features
  • Day 14: Review & self-quiz — focus on topics you missed from Week 1 practice test

Week 3 Latin American 20th Century (Days 15–21)

  • Day 15: Criollismo — Gallegos, Rivera, Güiraldes; Negrismo — Nicolás Guillén
  • Day 16: Borges — Ficciones, El Aleph, labyrinths, infinite time; his influence on the Boom
  • Day 17: Neruda — three phases; Mistral and Nobel Prize history; Paz's Laberinto de la soledad
  • Day 18: Magical realism deep dive — Carpentier's lo real maravilloso, Rulfo's Pedro Páramo, García Márquez
  • Day 19: The Boom — Vargas Llosa, Cortázar (Rayuela), Fuentes; Allende; testimonio (Menchú)
  • Day 20: Post-Boom — Bolaño, McOndo movement, contemporary voices
  • Day 21: Full Domain B self-quiz — practice test Latin American literature section

Week 4 Culture, Civilization & Final Review (Days 22–30)

  • Day 22: History of Spain — Moorish period, Reconquista, Inquisition, 1492
  • Day 23: Colonial Latin America — encomienda, mita, independence movements, key leaders
  • Day 24: Pre-Columbian civilizations — Aztec, Maya (Popol Vuh), Inca (quipu)
  • Day 25: Art — Velázquez, Goya, Picasso (Guernica), Gaudí, Rivera, Kahlo
  • Day 26: Music — flamenco, tango, cumbia, mariachi; cultural traditions and concepts (mestizaje, Día de los Muertos)
  • Day 27: Geography — Spain's regions and languages; Latin American countries and capitals
  • Day 28: Full 100-question practice test — timed, no interruptions
  • Day 29: Review all missed questions; identify any remaining weak domains; targeted re-study
  • Day 30: Light review only — constructed-response practice; rest and confidence-building

Adjust as needed: If you score above 80% on any domain in the Week 1 or 2 practice quiz, shift that time to weaker areas. Most candidates underestimate how much time the Culture & Civilization domain requires — don't leave it entirely for Week 4.

Constructed-Response Strategy

The two constructed-response questions are written in English and scored on a 1–4 scale. They typically ask you to analyze a literary work, compare two authors or movements, or discuss a cultural phenomenon in its historical context. Combined, they carry significant weight in your final scaled score.

What Good Responses Look Like

A scoring-level 3 or 4 response does four things: it directly addresses the prompt; it uses specific, accurate examples (titles, authors, dates, concepts); it demonstrates analytical thinking rather than only describing content; and it is well-organized with a clear argument. A vague response full of general statements and no specific textual or historical evidence will score 1–2.

Practice Prompts

Before exam day, practice writing timed responses to prompts like these:

  • Discuss how the Spanish Civil War shaped the work of one or more members of the Generation of '27.
  • Compare the treatment of national identity in the work of two Latin American authors from different countries or periods.
  • Explain how the encomienda system shaped colonial literature and indigenous cultural responses.
  • Discuss the role of magical realism as a cultural and political statement in the Latin American Boom.

Time management: Give yourself no more than 25 minutes per constructed-response question. Spend the first 3–4 minutes planning your argument and the examples you will use, then write. A clearly organized response with accurate specific examples is more valuable than a longer meandering one.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Confusing culteranismo and conceptismo

These two Baroque styles are frequently tested together. Culteranismo (Góngora) = ornate Latinized language and elaborate imagery. Conceptismo (Quevedo) = compressed wit, paradox, and intellectual wordplay. Know which author represents which, and know a defining example of each.

❌ Treating magical realism as a synonym for fantasy

Fantasy creates entirely secondary worlds. Magical realism presents magical events within a realistic social setting, narrated as ordinary, with the magic rooted in specific cultural worldviews (Caribbean oral tradition, indigenous cosmology). This distinction appears on almost every administration of the exam.

❌ Confusing the Generation of '98 with the Generation of '27

Generation of '98: response to Spain's colonial defeat (1898); philosophical, prose-heavy, nationalist, focused on Castile. Generation of '27: poetic generation, avant-garde, centered on the 300th anniversary of Góngora's death (1927). The Civil War connects the two — it ended the '27 generation's creative peak.

❌ Misidentifying Modernismo as a Spanish movement

Modernismo originated in Latin America, not Spain. Rubén Darío (Nicaragua) founded it. Spain had its own parallel development (Novecentismo, the Generation of '98) that was largely a reaction against Modernismo's aestheticism. Do not say "Modernismo is a Spanish movement."

❌ Ignoring the Culture & Civilization domain

Many candidates study literature extensively and barely touch the culture domain — then are surprised by questions about the quipu, Gaudí, or the Carnaval de Barranquilla. Domain C carries roughly the same weight as Domains A and B. Art history and pre-Columbian civilizations appear regularly.

❌ Studying for grammar instead of content

Subtest II does not test grammar. Time spent reviewing subjunctive triggers or verb conjugations is wasted for this exam. If you are preparing for both Subtest I and II simultaneously, maintain clear mental separation between the two content areas.

❌ Writing constructed responses without specific examples

Responses that say "García Márquez uses magic in his works to explore Latin American themes" with no specific title, character, event, or textual detail score in the 1–2 range. Always name specific works, characters, dates, and movements.

How to Use the Subtest II Practice Test

The free CSET Spanish Subtest II Practice Test has 100 questions covering all three domains — 70 standard multiple-choice, 20 passage-based reading questions, and 10 scenario-based analytical questions. Here is how to get the most out of it:

Use It Diagnostically First (Day 7–8 of Your Plan)

After your first week of studying, take the Domain A section without looking anything up. Your score will reveal exactly which literary periods or authors need more attention. Do the same with Domain B after Week 2. This prevents the common mistake of studying in a uniform way when your knowledge gaps are concentrated in specific areas.

Take the Full Test Timed on Day 28

Block two to three hours on Day 28 and take all 100 questions without interruption. This simulates exam conditions and reveals any stamina or time-management issues. Score yourself by domain: aim for 80%+ in each. Any domain below 70% needs targeted re-study on Day 29.

Read Every Explanation, Not Just the Ones You Missed

Lucky guesses can mask real knowledge gaps. Read the explanation for every question — even ones you answered correctly — because the explanations often contain related information that reinforces or extends what you just learned. This is especially valuable for the passage-based questions, where the explanations model the kind of literary analysis thinking the exam rewards.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is on CSET Spanish Subtest II?
CSET Spanish Subtest II (Test Code 146) covers Spanish literature, Latin American literature, literary movements and analysis, culture and civilization of Spain, and culture and civilization of Latin America — including geography, history, art, music, and cultural traditions. It does not test grammar, vocabulary, or speaking skills.
How is CSET Spanish Subtest II different from Subtest I?
Subtest I (145) tests active language skills: grammar, reading comprehension, vocabulary, and oral communication theory. Subtest II (146) tests content knowledge — what you know about literature and culture, not how well you use the language. The constructed-response questions on Subtest II are answered in English, not Spanish.
How many questions are on CSET Spanish Subtest II?
The actual exam contains approximately 52 selected-response (multiple-choice) questions and 2 constructed-response questions. The constructed-response questions ask you to write a short analytical essay in English about a literary or cultural topic. You have approximately 3 hours when taking Subtest II alone.
What is a passing score for CSET Spanish Subtest II?
The passing scaled score is 220 out of 300. Raw scores are converted to a scaled score, so the exact number of correct answers needed varies slightly by exam form. To build a safe buffer, aim for approximately 75–80% accuracy on selected-response questions combined with strong constructed-response scores (3 or 4 out of 4).
How hard is CSET Spanish Subtest II?
Most candidates find Subtest II more content-heavy than Subtest I because it requires memorizing a large body of literary and cultural knowledge spanning many centuries and two continents. Candidates with a humanities or Spanish literature background have a significant advantage. With 4–6 weeks of focused preparation using good resources, most serious candidates can pass. The pass rate is lower than Subtest I, so take preparation seriously.
Do I need to answer the constructed-response questions in Spanish?
No. The constructed-response questions on Subtest II are answered in English. You are being assessed on your knowledge of literature and culture, not your ability to write in Spanish. Writing in Spanish (composition and writing proficiency) is tested in Subtest I and Subtest III.
Can I take Subtest II without having passed Subtest I?
Yes. The three CSET Spanish subtests can be taken in any order and do not need to be taken on the same day. Many candidates take them separately, giving each subtest dedicated preparation time. However, you must pass all three to earn the credential.
