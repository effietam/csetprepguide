The CSET Spanish Subtest II passing score is 220 — but that number alone doesn't tell you much. This guide explains exactly how your score is calculated, what the 220 threshold actually means in practice, how constructed-response questions affect your final score, and what to do if you fall short.

The Passing Score: 220 out of 300

220 / 300
Passing Scaled Score — CSET Spanish Subtest II (Test Code 146)

Every CSET subtest uses a score scale of 100 to 300. The passing threshold for all three CSET Spanish subtests — including Subtest II — is a scaled score of 220. You need to reach 220 to pass; a 219 is a failing score regardless of how close it feels.

The 220 threshold was set by the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing (CTC) to represent the level of subject-matter knowledge expected of a fully qualified Spanish teacher. It is not an arbitrary number — it was established through a process called standard-setting in which panels of educators determined the minimum competency level required for classroom readiness.

A score of exactly 220 is a full pass — it grants the same credential as a score of 290. There is no benefit to scoring higher than 220 beyond the confidence it gives you on exam day. Your goal is simply to reach 220, not to maximize your score.

How Scaled Scoring Works

Raw scores — the actual number of questions you answered correctly — are never reported directly. Instead, they are converted to a scaled score between 100 and 300 using a process called equating. This conversion accomplishes two things:

1

Adjusts for exam difficulty

Different administrations of the CSET have slightly different questions of varying difficulty. Scaling ensures that a candidate who took a harder version of the exam is not penalized compared to someone who took an easier version — the scaled score reflects ability, not the luck of which exam form you received.

2

Combines selected-response and constructed-response into one score

Your raw count of correct multiple-choice answers and your 1–4 scores on the two constructed-response questions are weighted and combined into a single scaled score. The two components are not added together after the fact — the weighting is built into the scaling formula from the start.

Because of scaling, there is no fixed number of correct answers that guarantees a passing score. The conversion varies slightly between exam forms. This is why practice tests report percentages and domain breakdowns rather than telling you "get X questions right to pass."

Practical target: Aim for 75–80% accuracy on selected-response questions combined with a 3 or 4 on each constructed-response prompt. Candidates who hit both targets consistently pass. If your practice test scores consistently land above 80%, your buffer is strong.

Selected-Response Section

The selected-response section of Subtest II contains approximately 52 multiple-choice questions, each with four answer choices (A–D). Every question is worth the same number of raw points. There is no penalty for wrong answers — a blank and a wrong answer are equivalent, so always make your best guess on every question.

Question Types

Selected-response questions on Subtest II come in three main formats:

FormatWhat It AsksExample
Standalone knowledge questions Direct recall or application of literary and cultural knowledge "Which of the following best characterizes culteranismo as practiced by Góngora?"
Passage-based questions Reading a short excerpt and answering questions about its meaning, style, or historical context A passage from Sor Juana followed by questions on its literary devices or cultural significance
Scenario-based questions A teaching or analytical scenario requiring application of literary or cultural knowledge "A student argues that magical realism is simply fantasy fiction. Which response best corrects this misunderstanding?"

No partial credit: Each selected-response question is scored as correct or incorrect. You receive full credit for a correct answer and zero for an incorrect or blank answer. There is no deduction for guessing, so eliminate as many wrong answers as possible and always select your best choice.

Constructed-Response Scoring

Subtest II includes two constructed-response questions, each answered in English. These prompts ask you to demonstrate subject-matter expertise through written analysis — typically comparing two literary works or movements, analyzing a cultural phenomenon in historical context, or discussing the connections between language and culture.

The Scoring Rubric

Trained scorers evaluate each response on a 4-point scale using state-approved rubric criteria. If two scorers' ratings differ by more than one point, a third scorer adjudicates. Here is what each score level means in practice:

4
Strong

Thorough knowledge, specific and accurate examples, well-organized analysis, clear argument. No significant errors.

3
Adequate

Adequate knowledge with mostly accurate examples. Analysis is present but may lack depth or organization in places.

2
Limited

Limited or partially accurate knowledge. Examples are vague or sparse. Analysis is superficial or underdeveloped.

1
Weak

Significant deficiencies in content knowledge. Little or no supporting evidence. May misidentify key concepts.

How Much Do Constructed Responses Matter?

Constructed-response questions carry meaningful weight in the final scaled score — enough that strong essays can raise a borderline candidate above 220, and weak essays can push a candidate who did well on selected-response below the threshold. Think of the constructed-response section as your opportunity to add points, not just to avoid losing them.

A candidate who scores 3 or 4 on both constructed-response questions is in a significantly stronger position than one who scores 1–2, even if their selected-response accuracy is similar. Preparing for the constructed-response format specifically — not just studying content — is an important part of exam preparation.

Common mistake: Many candidates focus entirely on the multiple-choice section and barely practice writing constructed responses. This is a costly error. Practice writing at least three timed constructed-response essays before exam day, using specific titles, authors, dates, and cultural details in every response.

Reading Your Score Report

Official CSET score reports are released approximately 3–4 weeks after your exam date. You'll receive an email notification and can access the report in your Pearson account. Here is what the report contains and how to use it.

Overall scaled scoreYour total score on the 100–300 scale. Pass or fail is determined here.
Pass/Not Pass designationA clear pass or not-pass result for this subtest.
Domain performance indicatorsA relative performance indicator for each content domain (e.g., Spanish Literature, Latin American Literature, Culture & Civilization). Shown as ranges: Below Average, Average, Above Average.
Constructed-response scoresYour score (1–4) on each of the two constructed-response questions.

How to Interpret Domain Performance Indicators

The domain performance indicators do not show an exact percentage — they show a relative performance band for each content domain. Use them to identify where your score is dragging down your total. Here is how to read the three bands:

IndicatorWhat It MeansWhat to Do
Above Average You performed well in this domain relative to other candidates. This is a strength area. Maintain, but don't over-invest study time here before retaking.
Average You performed similarly to most other candidates. Competent but not a differentiator. Some targeted review is worthwhile, especially on high-frequency topics.
Below Average This domain is likely contributing most to a failing or borderline score. This is your top priority. Deep-dive study before retaking is essential.

Important: The domain performance indicators on the score report are relative indicators, not absolute percentages. "Average" does not mean you scored 50% — it means your performance in that domain was near the midpoint of all test takers. Focus your retake preparation on any domain marked Below Average first.

Score Validity

A passing CSET score does not expire. Once you pass a subtest, that score is permanently on record with the CTC. There is no deadline by which you must pass all three subtests — you can pass them in any order over any period of time, and your passing scores will be combined when you apply for your credential.

What to Do If You Don't Pass

A not-pass result on Subtest II is not unusual — the exam tests a large and specific body of content knowledge that most candidates need to study deliberately. Here is a clear four-step process for bouncing back effectively.

1

Read your score report carefully

Before scheduling your retake, download your full score report from your Pearson account and identify every domain marked Below Average. Also note your constructed-response scores — if either prompt scored 1 or 2, that area needs specific attention before you test again.

2

Build a targeted study plan, not a general one

If you failed because of weak Latin American literature knowledge and strong culture scores, spending equal time on all three domains is inefficient. Use the score report to build a study plan weighted toward your specific weaknesses. The Subtest II Study Guide is organized by domain to make this easy.

3

Practice constructed-response writing with specific examples

If your constructed-response scores were 1 or 2, generic study alone will not fix the problem. Practice writing timed responses (25 minutes each) to literary and cultural prompts, and ensure every response includes specific titles, author names, dates, movements, and analytical connections — not just general descriptions.

4

Retake when your practice scores consistently clear 80%

Use the free Subtest II Practice Test as your readiness benchmark. When you consistently score 80%+ across all three domains and can write a 3–4 level constructed response in 25 minutes, you are ready to retake. Retaking before you reach that threshold risks another not-pass result and an additional registration fee.

No waiting period: You can register to retake CSET Spanish Subtest II immediately after receiving your score — there is no mandatory waiting period between attempts and no limit on the number of times you can take any CSET subtest. The only constraint is the registration fee and exam availability at your preferred test center.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the passing score for CSET Spanish Subtest II?
The passing scaled score for CSET Spanish Subtest II (Test Code 146) is 220 out of a possible 300. This is the same passing threshold as Subtest I (145) and Subtest III (147).
How many questions do I need to get right to pass?
There is no fixed number of correct answers that guarantees a passing score because raw scores are converted to scaled scores, and the conversion varies slightly between exam forms. As a practical target, aim for approximately 75–80% accuracy on selected-response questions combined with a score of 3 or 4 on each constructed-response prompt. Candidates who consistently hit both targets in practice pass the real exam.
How is the CSET Spanish Subtest II score calculated?
Your raw score from the approximately 52 selected-response questions and your scores from the 2 constructed-response questions are weighted and combined into a single scaled score on a 100–300 scale. The selected-response section carries the majority of the weight. Constructed-response scores can meaningfully raise or lower your final result, so both components matter.
How are the constructed-response questions scored?
Each constructed-response question is scored by trained scorers on a 1–4 scale. A score of 4 demonstrates thorough subject-matter knowledge with well-supported analysis and specific examples. A score of 3 is adequate. Scores of 1 or 2 indicate significant deficiencies and will drag down your overall scaled score. Responses are written in English.
When do I get my CSET Spanish Subtest II score?
Official score reports are typically released within 3–4 weeks of your testing date. You will receive an email notification when your report is available in your Pearson account. A preliminary selected-response score may be available at the test center immediately after finishing the exam, but this is not your official score.
What happens if I don't pass CSET Spanish Subtest II?
You can retake CSET Spanish Subtest II at any time — there is no waiting period and no limit on the number of attempts. Your score report will show your performance on each content domain, which tells you exactly where to focus your preparation. Only the subtest you did not pass needs to be retaken; passing scores on other subtests remain valid indefinitely.
Does a failing score on Subtest II affect my other subtest scores?
No. Each CSET Spanish subtest is scored independently. A failing score on Subtest II does not affect your Subtest I or Subtest III scores in any way. You only need to retake the specific subtest you did not pass.
