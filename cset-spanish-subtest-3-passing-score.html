CSET Spanish Subtest III Passing Score: Everything Explained

📊 Test Code 147 🎯 Passing score: 220 📏 Scale: 100–300 📅 Updated 2026

The passing score for CSET Spanish Subtest III (Test Code 147) is a scaled score of 220 out of 300. But what does that actually mean in terms of questions right or wrong? This page breaks down exactly how your raw responses are converted to a scaled score, how the constructed-response questions affect your total, and what to do if you fall short.

⚡ Key Takeaways

  • The passing scaled score is 220 out of 300. Scores below 220 are failing; 220 or above is passing.
  • The score scale runs from 100 to 300 — you cannot score below 100 even with zero questions correct.
  • Roughly 70% accuracy on multiple-choice combined with average constructed-response scores typically reaches 220.
  • The two constructed-response questions each count significantly — weak essays can push a borderline MC score below passing.
  • Score reports include domain-level performance indicators so you know exactly where you were strong or weak.
  • If you fail, you must wait 45 days before retesting. There is no limit on total attempts.

A score of 220 is required to pass CSET Spanish Subtest III independently. This same threshold applies to all three CSET Spanish subtests (145, 146, and 147). Passing scores for all three subtests are required to earn the California Single Subject Teaching Credential in Spanish — there is no combined score averaging across subtests.

The 220 threshold was established by the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing (CTC) through a standard-setting process involving panels of educators who evaluated the minimum competency expected of a qualified Spanish teacher candidate. It represents a meaningful — but achievable — level of subject matter knowledge.

How Scaled Scores Work

CSET does not report your raw score (the number of questions you answered correctly). Instead, your raw responses are converted to a scaled score between 100 and 300 through a statistical process called equating. This ensures fairness across different test forms and administrations — a 220 on one test date represents the same level of performance as a 220 on any other date, even if one version was slightly harder.

Why Scaled Scores Matter for Borderline Candidates

Equating means that if you sit for a slightly more difficult version of Subtest III, fewer correct answers are required to reach 220 than on an easier version. Conversely, an easier form requires more correct answers. You cannot predict which version you will receive, so the practical implication is simple: aim well above the minimum threshold. Targeting approximately 75–80% accuracy gives you a meaningful buffer above 220.

Note: Pearson and CTC do not publish the exact conversion tables used for scaling. The 70% guideline is an estimate based on the structure of the exam and the 220/300 ratio. Actual conversions vary by form. The safest approach: prepare to know the material thoroughly, not just to reach the minimum.

Multiple-Choice vs. Constructed-Response Weight

CSET Spanish Subtest III contains two types of questions, and understanding how each is scored is critical for test strategy:

ComponentCountScoring MethodRelative Weight
Multiple-Choice Questions~50 questionsMachine-scored; 1 point each, no penalty for guessingApproximately 70% of total score
Constructed-Response Questions2 questionsScored by 2 trained human raters on a 1–4 scale each; scores combinedApproximately 30% of total score

How Constructed Responses Are Scored

Each constructed-response question is independently scored by two trained raters using a 4-point rubric. The two scores are added together, giving each question a possible range of 2–8 points. A score of 1 from a single rater represents a response that is seriously deficient; a 4 represents a response that fully addresses the prompt with accurate content, clear organization, and appropriate academic language.

If the two raters' scores differ by more than one point, a third rater resolves the discrepancy. Your combined constructed-response score is then factored into the overall scaled score alongside your multiple-choice performance.

Critical point: A candidate who answers multiple-choice questions at 75% accuracy but writes weak, underdeveloped constructed responses can still fail. Conversely, a candidate who scores slightly below 70% on multiple-choice but writes two strong constructed responses can still pass. Both sections matter — do not neglect written preparation.

Constructed-Response Scoring Criteria

While the official rubric is not published in full, CSET constructed-response scoring consistently evaluates:

CriterionWhat Raters Look For

Estimating Your Raw Score Target

Because Pearson does not publish exact raw-to-scaled conversion tables, the following estimates are based on the exam structure and the 220/300 passing standard. Use these as planning targets, not guarantees.

~75% correct (~37–38/50)Likely passing with average CR
~80%+ correct (~40+/50)Comfortable passing margin

At the borderline (approximately 70% MC accuracy), your constructed-response scores become decisive. Two well-developed, accurate responses scoring 3–4 from each rater can carry a borderline MC performance over the threshold. Two weak responses scoring 1–2 from each rater can cause a 72–73% MC performance to fall short.

There Is No Penalty for Guessing

CSET multiple-choice questions are scored on a right/wrong basis only — there is no deduction for incorrect answers. If you are unsure, always make your best guess rather than leaving a question blank. Eliminate any clearly wrong options first to improve your odds before guessing.

Reading Your Score Report

CSET score reports contain more than just a pass/fail result. Understanding every element helps you study more effectively on a retake.

Report ElementWhat It Means
Scaled ScoreYour overall score on a 100–300 scale. 220+ = pass.
Pass/Not PassOfficial determination. Only this designation is transmitted to CTC — the numerical score is for your reference.
Domain Performance IndicatorsFor each domain (Cultural Perspectives, Literary Analysis, Composition, Oral/Aural Theory), you receive a relative performance label: Below Average, Average, or Above Average. These are relative to the passing standard, not other test-takers.
Constructed-Response ScoresIndividual scores for each constructed-response question (combined rater scores), displayed alongside the MC performance.
Score ValidityScores do not expire while your credential application is active with CTC.

How to Use Domain Performance Indicators

If you receive a "Below Average" indicator for a domain, that area should be your first focus in any retake preparation. A candidate who passes Oral/Aural Theory questions but scores below average on Literary Analysis knows exactly where to direct study time. Use the domain indicators as a diagnostic — they are the most actionable information in the score report after a failed attempt.

If You Don't Pass

A failing score is a setback, not a permanent barrier. Here is what to do:

Step 1: Analyze Your Score Report

Before scheduling a retake, study your domain performance indicators carefully. Identify whether you were close overall (210–219) or significantly below the threshold (below 200). A close miss may need targeted strengthening of one or two domains; a larger gap needs a broader study reset.

Step 2: Address Constructed-Response Weaknesses

If your MC performance was strong but you still failed, weak constructed-response scores are likely the cause. Practice writing timed academic essays in Spanish before your retake. Focus on: clear arguable thesis, organized body paragraphs with specific evidence, formal academic register, and appropriate use of Spanish discourse connectors. Get feedback from a colleague or instructor if possible.

Step 3: Schedule Your Retake

You must wait at least 45 calendar days before retaking a CSET subtest you did not pass. Register through ctcexams.nesinc.com. The same registration fee applies. Give yourself at least 30 days of focused preparation after the waiting period ends before sitting again.

Step 4: Study Strategically, Not Just More

Repeating the same preparation that produced a failing score is unlikely to produce a passing one. Use the domain indicators to shift your focus. If you were weak in SLA theory, dedicate structured time specifically to Krashen, Vygotsky, Swain, Long, and Cummins. If literary analysis was weak, read and analyze actual Spanish-language passages — not just lists of author names.

Good news: Candidates who scored 210–219 on their first attempt — very close to passing — typically pass on a single focused retake. The domain indicators tell you exactly where to invest the additional preparation time.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the passing score for CSET Spanish Subtest III? ▾
The passing scaled score for CSET Spanish Subtest III (Test Code 147) is 220 out of a possible 300. Scores are reported on a scale of 100 to 300. A score of 220 or higher is required to pass this subtest.
How many questions do I need to get right to pass? ▾
There is no fixed raw score threshold because scores are scaled. As a general guideline, answering approximately 70–75% of multiple-choice questions correctly and earning average scores on both constructed-response questions typically places candidates near or above the 220 passing threshold. The constructed-response questions carry significant weight — approximately 30% of the total score.
How long does it take to receive CSET Spanish Subtest III scores? ▾
Score reports are typically available approximately 3 to 5 weeks after the test date. Scores are released on scheduled score release dates published by Pearson at ctcexams.nesinc.com. You will receive an email notification when your scores are available in your online account.
What happens if I fail CSET Spanish Subtest III? ▾
If you do not pass, you must wait at least 45 days before retaking the subtest. There is no limit on the number of attempts. Your score report will include domain performance indicators identifying your weak areas. Subtests you have already passed remain valid while your credential application is active.
Do CSET scores expire? ▾
CSET scores do not expire as long as you are actively pursuing a California teaching credential. If you have not completed your credential program or filed an application for an extended period, confirm current CTC policy at ctc.ca.gov, as policies can change.
Is the passing score the same for all three CSET Spanish subtests? ▾
Yes. All three CSET Spanish subtests — Subtest I (145), Subtest II (146), and Subtest III (147) — require a passing scaled score of 220 out of 300. Each subtest is passed or failed independently; there is no averaging across subtests.
Can I see my score immediately after the exam? ▾
No. Because Subtest III includes constructed-response questions that must be scored by human raters, scores cannot be reported immediately. You will receive your score report approximately 3–5 weeks after your test date on the next scheduled score release date.
